Core rules

Main rule

Acidity depends on the stability of the conjugate base.

A more stable conjugate base means a stronger acid.

Two main stabilising factors

  • electron-withdrawing inductive effects (−I)
  • resonance delocalisation of negative charge

Scope of these notes

These notes compare:

  • chlorocarboxylic and bromocarboxylic acids
  • benzoic acid and ethanoic acid
  • phenol, water, and ethanol

The aim is to explain acid strength using structure and electronic effects rather than memorisation alone.

Main section

Acidity comparisons

Lower pKa = stronger acid

1. Chlorocarboxylic acid, ClCH2COOH

pKa: 2.86

This is the strongest acid in this set.

Why it is strong

  • chlorine exerts a strong −I effect
  • it pulls electron density away through sigma bonds
  • the conjugate base is also stabilised by resonance across the two oxygen atoms

The negative charge in the conjugate base is both reduced by the electron-withdrawing chlorine atom and delocalised over the carboxylate group.

2. Bromocarboxylic acid, BrCH2COOH

pKa: 2.90

This is also a strong acid, but slightly weaker than the chloro analogue.

Why it is slightly weaker

  • bromine also gives a −I effect
  • but it is weaker than chlorine
  • the conjugate base is still stabilised by carboxylate resonance

Both compounds are strongly stabilised by the carboxylate group, but chlorine withdraws electron density more strongly than bromine.

3. Benzoic acid, C6H5COOH

pKa: 4.20

This is moderately acidic.

Why it is stronger than ethanoic acid

  • the conjugate base is stabilised by delocalisation over the carboxylate group
  • the benzene ring contributes a mild withdrawing effect through the attached sp2 system

The carboxylate group already provides strong stabilisation. Compared with an alkyl group, the phenyl group is less electron-donating and gives a better environment for acidity.

4. Ethanoic acid, CH3COOH

pKa: 4.76

This is weaker than benzoic acid.

Why it is weaker

  • the conjugate base is stabilised by resonance over the two oxygen atoms
  • the methyl group has a weak +I effect
  • this slightly destabilises the negative charge

Resonance still makes ethanoic acid much more acidic than alcohols, but the methyl group makes it weaker than benzoic acid.

5. Phenol, C6H5OH

pKa: 10.0

This is a weak acid.

Why it is more acidic than ethanol

  • after losing H+, the phenoxide ion forms
  • the negative charge can be delocalised into the aromatic ring
  • this gives stabilisation that is not available in an alkoxide ion

Phenol is much less acidic than carboxylic acids, but clearly more acidic than ethanol because phenoxide is resonance-stabilised.

6. Water, H2O

pKa: 14.0

This is a useful reference point.

Why it sits in the middle of weak acids

  • hydroxide has the negative charge localised on one oxygen atom
  • there is no extra stabilisation from resonance
  • there is also no alkyl group pushing extra electron density towards oxygen

Water is a helpful baseline for comparing the acidities of phenol and alcohols.

7. Ethanol, CH3CH2OH

pKa: 16.0

This is the weakest acid in this set.

Why it is so weak

  • the ethyl group has a +I effect
  • it pushes electron density towards the oxygen
  • this destabilises the conjugate base, ethoxide

Because the negative charge remains localised on oxygen and is pushed further by the alkyl group, ethanol is a very weak acid.

Acidity order

Strongest to weakest:
ClCH2COOH > BrCH2COOH > C6H5COOH > CH3COOH > C6H5OH > H2O > CH3CH2OH

Acidity comparison summary

Why carboxylic acids are much stronger than phenols and alcohols

Carboxylic acids form carboxylate ions.

In a carboxylate ion, the negative charge is delocalised over two oxygen atoms. This is a very effective stabilisation.

Phenoxide ions are also stabilised, but the delocalisation is less effective than in carboxylates. Alkoxide ions are not resonance-stabilised at all.

Why chlorine-substituted carboxylic acids are especially strong

A halogen such as chlorine pulls electron density away by induction.

This makes the conjugate base less electron-dense and more stable. The stronger the −I effect, the stronger the acid.

Exam-focused comparisons

Be ready to explain:

  • why chlorocarboxylic acid is stronger than bromocarboxylic acid
  • why benzoic acid is stronger than ethanoic acid
  • why phenol is stronger than ethanol
  • why carboxylic acids are stronger than phenols and alcohols

Common reasoning pattern

For acidity questions

How stable is the conjugate base?

  • −I effects
  • resonance delocalisation
  • reduced charge density
  • where the negative charge is located

Final takeaway

Stronger acids usually have

  • a more stable conjugate base
  • stronger −I effects
  • better resonance stabilisation

The most reliable way to compare acids is not to memorise isolated examples, but to connect structure to charge stability.
