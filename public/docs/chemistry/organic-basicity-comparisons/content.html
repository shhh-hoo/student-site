Core rules

Main rule

Basicity depends on the availability of the lone pair.

A more available lone pair means a stronger base.

Two main controlling factors

  • electron-donating inductive effects (+I)
  • delocalisation, which reduces lone-pair availability

Scope of these notes

These notes compare:

  • ethylamine and ammonia
  • phenylamine and ethanamide
  • water as a weak base reference point

The aim is to explain base strength using lone-pair availability and structure, rather than isolated memorisation.

Basicity comparisons

Lower pKb = stronger base

1. Ethylamine, CH3CH2NH2

pKb: 3.30

This is the strongest base in this set.

Why it is strong

  • the ethyl group has a +I effect
  • it increases electron density on nitrogen
  • the lone pair becomes more available to bond to H+

An alkyl group makes the nitrogen lone pair easier to donate, so alkylamines are stronger bases than ammonia.

2. Ammonia, NH3

pKb: 4.75

This is a useful reference base.

Why it is moderately basic

  • the lone pair is localised on nitrogen
  • there is no strong electron-donating or electron-withdrawing group attached

Ammonia is less basic than ethylamine because it lacks extra +I support from an alkyl group.

3. Phenylamine (aniline), C6H5NH2

pKb: 9.40

This is a weak base.

Why it is weaker than ammonia and ethylamine

  • the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised into the benzene ring
  • this reduces its availability for protonation

Delocalisation makes the lone pair less available, so phenylamine is much weaker as a base than an alkylamine.

4. Ethanamide, CH3CONH2

pKb: 14.0

This is extremely weak as a base.

Why it is so weak

  • the nitrogen lone pair is strongly delocalised towards the carbonyl group
  • resonance gives the C–N bond partial double-bond character
  • the lone pair is therefore much less available

Amides are much weaker bases than amines because the nitrogen lone pair is tied into resonance with the carbonyl group.

5. Water, H2O

pKb: 15.7

This is a very weak base.

Why it is weak

  • oxygen is highly electronegative
  • it holds its lone pairs tightly
  • proton acceptance is less favourable than for nitrogen bases

Water can act as a base, but it is far weaker than typical amines.

Basicity order

Strongest to weakest:
CH3CH2NH2 > NH3 > C6H5NH2 > CH3CONH2 > H2O

Basicity comparison summary

Why alkylamines are stronger bases than ammonia

Alkyl groups donate electron density by the +I effect.

This increases the availability of the nitrogen lone pair.

Why phenylamine is weaker than alkylamines

In phenylamine, the lone pair can be delocalised into the benzene ring.

This makes it less available to bond to H+.

Why amides are much weaker bases than amines

In an amide, the nitrogen lone pair is delocalised towards the carbonyl group.

This resonance effect is strong, so the lone pair is much less available for protonation.

Exam-focused comparisons

Be ready to explain:

  • why ethylamine is more basic than ammonia
  • why phenylamine is less basic than ethylamine
  • why amides are much weaker bases than amines
  • why lone-pair delocalisation weakens basicity

Common reasoning pattern

For basicity questions

How available is the lone pair?

  • +I effects
  • resonance delocalisation
  • whether the lone pair is localised or shared with another system

Final takeaway

Stronger bases usually have

  • a more available lone pair
  • stronger +I effects
  • less delocalisation away from the basic atom

The most reliable way to compare bases is not to memorise isolated examples, but to connect structure to lone-pair availability.
